# Activity: Procurement Strategy — Buy AI Access or Buy AI Embedded?

**Module 5 | Estimated time: 10–12 minutes**

---

## What This Activity Is About

When a software vendor says their product now has "built-in AI," that's a procurement decision waiting to happen — not just a feature announcement. The choice between buying AI that's woven into a specific platform versus buying AI access that works across your tools has real consequences for cost, flexibility, and control.

In this activity, you'll read a fictional vendor pitch and analyze it using concepts from Module 5.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this activity, you will be able to:

- Describe in plain language how MCP-based AI integration differs from AI embedded in a vendor platform
- Identify what a vendor gains control over when AI is built into their product
- Articulate the switching costs and capability risks of each approach
- Frame smart questions a decision-maker should ask before signing

## Framework Reminder

Before reading the pitch below, here's the core distinction to keep in mind:

**Buying AI embedded in a platform** means the AI capability lives inside the vendor's product. The vendor controls what the AI can do, what data it sees, how it improves over time, and what happens to that capability if you stop paying or switch products.

**Buying AI access** (such as through an MCP-based approach) means the AI capability is separate from any single platform. The AI can connect to multiple tools through standardized interfaces, and you keep that capability even if you swap out one of those tools.

The key question in any AI procurement decision is: **who ends up owning the intelligence layer?**

## The Vendor Pitch

Read this carefully before answering the analysis questions.

---

> "We're excited to introduce SalesForge AI — the new version of our CRM platform with built-in AI that drafts follow-up emails, summarizes call transcripts, flags at-risk deals, and suggests next best actions — all without leaving your SalesForge dashboard. Our AI learns your team's patterns and gets smarter over time. Upgrade to SalesForge AI Pro and let the platform work for you."

---

## Your Analysis Questions

Answer all three questions in writing. A few well-developed sentences per question is enough — you don't need an essay.

---

### Question 1 — Vendor Lock-In Analysis

If your organization buys SalesForge AI Pro, what does the vendor now control that they didn't before?

Think through:
- What data is the AI learning from?
- Where does that learning live?
- If you decide to switch CRMs in two years, what happens to your AI capability — the patterns it learned, the summaries it generated, the workflows it supported?

---

### Question 2 — The MCP Alternative

Describe in plain terms how an MCP-based approach to the same problem would be structured differently.

Use these three concepts in your answer:
- **Client** — the AI agent or assistant doing the work
- **Server** — the MCP server connecting the agent to a specific tool or data source
- **Capability manifest** — the declared list of what the agent is allowed to do through that connection

In an MCP-based setup, what would you keep if you switched CRMs? What changes and what stays?

*You don't need to know how to build an MCP server — describe the structure the way you'd explain it to a department head who's never heard the term.*

---

### Question 3 — Your Recommendation

Based on your analysis, what would you recommend to a department head considering the SalesForge AI Pro upgrade?

You don't need to say yes or no outright. What questions should they ask the vendor before signing? What should they try to find out about data ownership, portability, and what happens if they cancel?

## Worked Example

Here is a completed answer for **Question 1 only** — to model the expected depth of analysis. Questions 2 and 3 are yours to work through.

---

**Question 1 — Worked example:**

> If we buy SalesForge AI Pro, we're not just buying features — we're handing the vendor ongoing access to our sales data as training material. The AI "learns our team's patterns" using our deal history, our email language, our pipeline behavior. That learning lives inside SalesForge's infrastructure, not ours. If we switch CRMs in two years, we'd be starting from scratch with a new AI that knows nothing about how our team works. More importantly, we'd be walking away from the value we created — every pattern the model learned, every workflow it refined — because we never owned any of it. We also don't know what SalesForge does with those learned patterns at the platform level: do they use aggregate customer data to improve the product for all customers? The pitch doesn't say. Before signing, I'd want to know exactly what data the AI is trained on, where that training data lives, who owns it contractually, and what we get (if anything) if we cancel.
