## Who Gets to Decide? Human Oversight in an Age of Autonomous Agents

### Overview

By this point in the course, you have built an agent that can read documents, retrieve information, and act on your behalf. You have seen it work. If your experience matches most, you have also seen it get something wrong in a way you did not expect. That experience is exactly the right setup for the question this discussion asks:

*When an AI agent takes an action and that action causes harm, who is responsible?*

This is not a hypothetical. In 2024, a Canadian court ruled that Air Canada was legally liable for a refund policy its own chatbot invented and told a grieving passenger, even though the airline argued the chatbot was a "separate legal entity" responsible for its own statements. The court disagreed. The company owned the agent. The company owned the outcome.

As agents move from answering questions to taking actions, placing orders, sending messages, modifying records, issuing refunds, the stakes of that accountability question grow. And unlike a chatbot that gives wrong information, an action-taking agent can compound a mistake faster than any human reviewer can catch it.

McKinsey partner Rich Isenberg frames it this way: agency is not a feature. It is a transfer of decision rights. The question is no longer whether the model is accurate. The question is who is accountable when the system acts.

The challenge every organization now faces is the same one you face as you think about deploying what you have built: how much authority do you give the agent, and where does a human have to stay in the loop? Give the agent too little autonomy and you lose most of the benefit. Give it too much and you lose control over outcomes your name is attached to.

There is no universally correct answer. The right amount of autonomy depends on your industry, your risk tolerance, the reversibility of the actions involved, and the cost of a mistake. This discussion asks you to work through that question for your own context, and to engage with how your classmates are approaching it in theirs.

### Readings

There are two readings for this discussion. Both are free and publicly accessible.

**Reading 1 — "Trust in the Age of Agents"**

McKinsey and Company | March 2026 | 15-minute read (podcast transcript)

🔗 [Read or listen at mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/risk-and-resilience/our-insights/trust-in-the-age-of-agents)

McKinsey partner Rich Isenberg walks through what it actually means to give software the ability to plan, act, and make decisions on behalf of an organization.

What to focus on: The section where Isenberg describes three tiers of agent autonomy. Low-autonomy agents act as copilots that answer questions, where the main risk is inaccuracy. Semi-autonomous agents operate within bounded rules, like procurement or invoice approval, where the risk is an inappropriate approval that carries financial consequences. Fully autonomous agents make system changes and infrastructure decisions without human sign-off, where the risk is scope and containment. Ask yourself where your own agent use case sits on that spectrum. Also read the section near the end where Isenberg lists five questions he says any board should be able to answer with precision before an agent goes to production. How many of those questions could your organization answer right now?

**Reading 2 — "Air Canada Must Honor Refund Policy Its Chatbot Invented"**

*Moffatt v. Air Canada*, British Columbia Civil Resolution Tribunal | February 2024

Several outlets covered this case in depth. Search: *Air Canada chatbot refund court ruling 2024 Moffatt*. You will find coverage from CBC News, the American Bar Association, Ars Technica, and others. Any is a valid source.

🔗 [CBC News coverage](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/british-columbia/air-canada-chatbot-lawsuit-1.7116416)

What to focus on: The tribunal's reasoning for holding Air Canada, not the chatbot, accountable for the misinformation. Air Canada argued its chatbot was a "separate legal entity responsible for its own actions." The tribunal rejected this entirely. How does that reasoning change when the agent is not just saying things but doing things: modifying records, sending messages, approving transactions? Who in your organization would be the equivalent of Air Canada in this scenario, and do they know it?

### Your Post

Write a post of 400 to 500 words that addresses all three of the following:

**1. Your tier and your line**

Describe a specific task or workflow where you can imagine deploying an agent in your own work or organization. Using the three-tier framework from the McKinsey reading, identify which tier it belongs in and briefly explain why. Then describe where you would draw the line: the point at which the agent must stop and ask a human before proceeding. Be specific. "When the stakes are high" is not a line. "Before sending any external communication" or "before modifying any record that affects a customer account" is a line.

**2. What makes a mistake reversible or not**

Using your own workplace as the context, give one example of an agent action that would be reversible and one that would not. Explain what makes the difference. This does not need to be dramatic. A reversible mistake might be a draft email that was never sent. An irreversible one might be a price change published to the website before anyone caught it.

**3. Your reaction to the Air Canada ruling**

The tribunal held the company, not the chatbot, responsible. Do you think that is the right outcome? Should the standard be different when the agent is taking actions rather than just providing information? Would your answer change if the agent had been given explicit permission by a human before taking the action that caused harm?
