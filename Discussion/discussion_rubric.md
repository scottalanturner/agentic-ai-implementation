# Discussion Question — Grading Rubric



| **Criteria** | **Novice** | **Competent** | **Proficient** |
|--------------|------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Reading engagement** | **Points: 10** — Post makes no identifiable reference to either reading, or references them only in passing ("as the readings said") without connecting any specific concept to the student's argument. | **Points: 18** — Post references at least one reading with a named concept, but the connection is surface-level. For example, the student mentions "tiered autonomy" without placing their agent in a specific tier, or cites the Air Canada outcome without applying its reasoning to their own context. | **Points: 25** — Post explicitly applies the McKinsey tier framework to the student's own agent use case with a brief explanation of why it belongs in that tier. Post also uses the Air Canada ruling to support or complicate the student's position on accountability, not just to summarize the facts. Both readings leave a visible mark on the argument. |
| **Specificity of examples** | **Points: 12** — Personal line is vague ("when something important is happening" or "when it could cause harm"); reversible and irreversible examples are generic or missing; the agent use case described could apply to any industry. | **Points: 22** — All three prompts attempted. The personal line names a condition but still leaves room for interpretation ("before contacting a customer"). Reversible and irreversible examples are present but one is thin or hypothetical rather than drawn from actual work. | **Points: 30** — The personal line is a testable condition a developer could implement: it names a specific action, threshold, or decision type. Both the reversible and irreversible examples come from the student's actual work context and the explanation of what makes them different is grounded in consequence, not just intuition. |
| **Argument quality** | **Points: 8** — Post restates the prompts or hedges every claim ("it depends," "there are many factors"). Student does not take a clear position on the Air Canada ruling or the accountability question. | **Points: 14** — Student takes a position on the Air Canada ruling and supports it with at least one reason. The argument is readable but either the reasoning is thin or the student abandons the position when complicating factors are raised in the same post. | **Points: 20** — Student takes a clear position and holds it. The Air Canada answer directly engages with the "actions vs. information" distinction raised in the prompt. If the student acknowledges a complication, they address it rather than letting it dissolve the argument. The post reads as a coherent piece of thinking, not a list of answers to three separate questions. |
| **Replies** | **Points: 10** — Fewer than two replies posted, or replies are fewer than 75 words each, or replies consist of agreement and restatement without any challenge or extension. | **Points: 14** — Two replies posted at adequate length. Each engages with the classmate's specific example or argument, but the challenge or extension is mild: it raises a question without pursuing it, or agrees with one part while noting a minor concern. | **Points: 20** — Two replies that push back or build in a way the classmate has to reckon with. The reply either challenges the tier classification with a reason, identifies a real-world scenario where the proposed line breaks down, or extends the argument to a higher-stakes version and explains what changes. The classmate's actual words or examples are referenced, not just the general topic. |
